Constitutional Literacy Module

Preamble · Rights · Duties · DPSP · Governance

The Preamble — Soul of the Constitution

The Preamble serves as the introduction to the Indian Constitution, encapsulating the ideals and aspirations of the nation. It declares India to be a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic committed to securing Justice, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity for all citizens.

"WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens: JUSTICE, social, economic and political; LIBERTY of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship; EQUALITY of status and of opportunity; and to promote among them all FRATERNITY assuring the dignity of the individual and the unity and integrity of the Nation..."

Sovereign — India is independent and not under any foreign control. Socialist — The state seeks to reduce inequalities of income and status. Secular — The state has no official religion; all religions are treated equally. Democratic — Government is elected by the people. Republic — Head of state is elected, not hereditary.

Core Values of the Preamble

Justice encompasses social justice (elimination of caste, gender, religious discrimination), economic justice (equitable distribution of resources), and political justice (equal voting rights, right to contest elections).

Liberty guarantees freedom of thought, expression, belief, faith, and worship — not absolute but reasonable and subject to constitutional limits.

Equality ensures equal status and opportunity for all citizens regardless of religion, caste, sex, or place of birth.

Fraternity promotes a sense of brotherhood among all citizens, reinforcing dignity of individuals and unity of the nation.

Fundamental Rights (Articles 12–35)

The Constitution guarantees six fundamental rights that protect citizens against arbitrary state actions:

1. Right to Equality (Art. 14–18)

Guarantees equality before law, prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex or place of birth, ensures equality of opportunity in public employment, abolishes untouchability, and prohibits titles except military/academic.

2. Right to Freedom (Art. 19–22)

Includes freedom of speech and expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, and profession. Also protects against arbitrary arrest through right to life and personal liberty (Art. 21).

3. Right Against Exploitation (Art. 23–24)

Prohibits trafficking, forced labour, and child labour in hazardous industries (below age 14).

4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Art. 25–28)

Guarantees freedom of conscience and free profession, practice and propagation of religion. Allows religious communities to manage religious affairs.

5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Art. 29–30)

Protects the rights of minorities to conserve their culture, language, and script, and to establish and administer educational institutions.

6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art. 32)

Called the "heart and soul of the Constitution" by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, this right allows citizens to move the Supreme Court directly for enforcement of fundamental rights through writs like Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Certiorari, Prohibition, and Quo Warranto.

Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

Added by the 42nd Amendment (1976), there are 11 Fundamental Duties. These are moral obligations of every citizen. While not legally enforceable, they reflect the responsibilities that come with citizenship:

  1. Abide by the Constitution and respect national flag & anthem
  2. Cherish and follow the noble ideals of the freedom struggle
  3. Uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India
  4. Defend the country and render national service when called upon
  5. Promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood
  6. Value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture
  7. Protect and improve the natural environment
  8. Develop scientific temper, humanism and spirit of inquiry and reform
  9. Safeguard public property and abjure violence
  10. Strive towards excellence in all individual and collective activity
  11. Provide opportunities for education to children (6–14 years) [added 2002]

Significance in Daily Life

Fundamental duties bridge the gap between rights and responsibilities. A responsible citizen not only exercises their rights but also fulfills duties. Examples: paying taxes honestly (duty 10), not destroying public property (duty 9), promoting communal harmony (duty 5), and encouraging children's education (duty 11).

Directive Principles of State Policy (Art. 36–51)

DPSPs are guidelines to the central and state governments for establishing a just society. Though not justiciable (non-enforceable by courts), they are fundamental to governance. Borrowed from Ireland's constitution, they aim to establish social and economic democracy.

Key Directives

Socio-Economic (Socialistic): Equal pay for equal work (Art. 39d), free legal aid (Art. 39A), right to work and education (Art. 41), living wage for workers (Art. 43), early childhood care (Art. 45).

Gandhian Principles: Organize village panchayats (Art. 40), promote cottage industries, prohibit cow slaughter (Art. 48), promote welfare of SC/ST (Art. 46).

Liberal-Intellectual: Uniform Civil Code (Art. 44), separate judiciary from executive (Art. 50), protect monuments (Art. 49), foster international peace (Art. 51).

Structure of Indian Governance

India follows a federal system with unitary features. The Constitution provides for three branches of government:

Legislature: Parliament (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha) at the centre, State Assemblies at state level. Makes laws.

Executive: President (constitutional head), Prime Minister & Council of Ministers (actual power). Implements laws.

Judiciary: Supreme Court, High Courts, District Courts. Interprets laws and protects fundamental rights.

Three-Tier Government & PRIs

India has a three-tier structure: Central, State, and Local. The 73rd & 74th Amendments (1992) gave constitutional status to Panchayati Raj Institutions (PRIs) and Urban Local Bodies, bringing democracy to the grassroots. This enables local self-governance and citizen participation at village level.
